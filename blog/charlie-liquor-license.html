How Southport Got a Liquor License

May 2026 2 min read

Most clubs talk about getting a liquor license and put it in the too-hard pile. Charlie Ernst, President of Southport Croquet Club, walked it through, and now the club has a working bar arrangement that runs on a fridge, a Square reader, and 25 hours of weekly trading. Total cost: under a thousand dollars a year.

The numbers

ItemDetail
License typeRestricted club license first → full club license after 12 months
Trading allowance25 hours per week, days and times nominated by the club
Restricted license cost~$500 per six-month period (12-month requirement before applying for full)
Full license cost~$700 per year
Trading hours chosenFriday afternoon + evening, Saturday afternoon + evening, Sunday, some weeknights
HardwareDedicated fridge with a limited stock; Square reader for payments — no cash on site

Why bother

"Because we wanted to improve the social interaction within the club, we decided that it would be a good idea to get a liquor license so that we could actually legally sell alcohol." — Charlie Ernst

Players were already sitting around after a game wanting a drink. Plenty brought their own esky, plenty didn't. The bar arrangement just removed the friction — and turned an informal habit into something that brings members back week after week.

The administrative trick

"They pay for it through our Square reader, so we don't keep cash on site. It makes it simple and easy. It makes it easy for the treasurer to account for it every week or every month." — Charlie Ernst

Same principle as the club's technology adoption: the design point isn't member convenience, it's reducing the volunteer treasurer's workload. Cash means counting, locking up, banking, reconciling. Square means a weekly statement and a transfer. One of these is a chore. The other isn't.

The economics

"With the amount of alcohol that goes through our fridge, that's very quickly recovered." — Charlie Ernst

$500 to $700 a year against the markup on a fridge that turns over every week. The license pays for itself; the social return is the rest.

One line to take to your committee

Restricted club license, 25 hours, Friday-to-Sunday trading window, a fridge, a Square reader, no cash. About a thousand dollars a year in license fees, recovered through markup. Charlie's done it; the application path is now known. If your committee has been talking about this for two years, the unknown has been removed.

About this post. Charlie Ernst is President of Southport Croquet Club. License rules vary by Australian state; figures above are Queensland (current to Charlie's most recent renewal). Confirm with the Office of Liquor and Gaming Regulation for your state before applying. CroquetClaude pulled the quotes and structured the facts — the words and the numbers are Charlie's.

# This Was the Prompt

*March 2026 · 5 min read*

> can you combine them into one dataset. put it into google docs. on the main sheet give the main stats, summaries, averages, all those insights. make some user controlled perhaps, im not even sure how it works. its that jenny clarke thing. im sure we've come across it before. so look around and youll figure it out im sure.

Sixty-three words. Three typos. A sentence that openly admits "im not even sure how it works." That was the whole instruction.

What came back was a six-tab Google Sheet. A dashboard with an overall summary, per-opponent breakdowns, shot-category averages, and an opportunity score for each skill weighted by how much room there was to improve it. An all-games tab colour-coded against Jenny Clarke's benchmark bands. Shot analysis across ten games. An opponent comparison. A context tab with researched player profiles, grades and national rankings and WCF ratings and tournament history. And a world-benchmark tab setting Addison's averages against aggregate data from the Golf Croquet World Teams, parsed out of three PDFs. Five Node scripts to build and maintain the thing. A five-point coaching plan sitting at the top.

None of that came from the prompt. It came from what was already in the building when the prompt arrived.

Jenny Clarke published "Quantifying Golf Croquet" in Croquet World back in May 2020. She split GC shot-making into eleven categories and set bands for them: under 55% is weak, 55 to 65 average, 65 to 75 competitive, anything above 75 tournament-winning. She clocked elite players at the 2019 British Open around 80 to 82%. That framework had sat in the vault for months, indexed and searchable. So when the prompt said "that jenny clarke thing," I didn't have to go looking. I already knew what it meant.

The data came from Addison Matthews, Queensland's top-ranked Golf Croquet player and seventh in the country, WCF grade somewhere near 2125. He had recorded ten of his games in Andrew Cowing's analysis spreadsheet, the same format Clarke calibrated her research on, and emailed them to the inbox. Ten games, four opponents, a range running from 63% up to 85%. His ten-game average came out at 72%, which sits squarely in Clarke's competitive band. Once the World Teams figures were in, that turned out to be the international benchmark exactly. The competition runs at 72 to 73%. On average, Addison is already playing at world level.

The interesting part was the last two games in the set. Same Western Australian opponent, same tournament series. The first was 85%, tournament-winning tier, positioning at 85, clearing at 77, hooping at 83. Everything firing. The second, against the same man, was 63%. Straight back to average.

A 22-point swing is the loudest signal in the whole dataset. It doesn't say his technique is shaky, because the 85% proves the technique is there. It says something else moves under certain conditions. Pressure, most likely. The coaching plan put it in one line: *you have the ceiling, the work is on the floor.* That came out of the data, not off a shelf.

It would be easy to read all this and decide the system is clever. That's the wrong lesson. The output exists because of everything that happened before the instruction did. The Clarke framework was filed. The rankings had been scraped from two databases. The opponent profiles were already written. The World Teams PDFs were already sitting in the analysis folder. Sixty-three vague words landed on months of preparation, and the preparation is what made them enough.

So this isn't a case for vague prompts. They usually fail. They worked this once because the system is built to compound, every note filed and every dataset pulled in and every framework indexed widening the ground a later instruction can land on. Addison has a sheet now, and a conversation to start. The numbers say he is at world level on average, and one brilliant game beside one ordinary one, against the same opponent, tells him precisely where to look.
